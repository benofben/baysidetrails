History

This timeline documents the Bayside Trails from early Tacoma maps through the modern restoration effort. Source materials include city/state documents, public records requests, archived media, and neighborhood fieldwork.

Full local archive of original History page images: History Image Archive.

1893

The topography of Tacoma was very different in 1893. Garfield Park didn’t exist. Instead, Garfield Gulch continued up to where the Tacoma Lawn Tennis Club would eventually be built. Saw mills were spaced along Commencement Bay.

Stadium Bowl didn’t exist yet. Old Woman’s Gulch was much bigger, and a railway ran along the slope above the bay. Today that alignment is the railroad grade section of the Bayside Trail.

1920

By 1920, the original railroad line appears abandoned and a new line ran along the bay next to Bayside Road. Schuster Parkway had not yet been built.

Further up the hill, the Tacoma Tennis Club and Annie Wright had been built, and Park Drive wrapped around a partially filled Garfield Gulch.

1962

Aerial photographs from 1962 show the area before Schuster Parkway existed.

1966

Aerial imagery from 1966 still shows the rail line and grain towers, with Schuster Parkway not yet built.

1973

On December 27, 1973, the City of Tacoma applied for a Land & Water Conservation Fund grant. The Bayside Trails project cost was slated at $138,720, with $69,360 covered by grant funds.

Grant detail: consolidated grant document and RCO project snapshot.

As part of this funding, Tacoma is required to keep the park open indefinitely or replace it with parkland of equal value.

1974

Work began on the Bayside Trail. Construction photos captured both Schuster Parkway and trail development.

1975

On May 16, 1975, Tacoma completed work on the Bayside Trails. The grand opening occurred May 17, 1975.

Construction record: project buildout document.

1977

Tacoma News Tribune reported the city won an award for the Bayside Trails. Black-and-white photos from January 29, 1977 documented the trail in active use.

1979

The City of Tacoma published a brochure describing the Bayside Trails. Brochure: Bayside Trails 1979 brochure.

1981

A January 11, 1981 newspaper story described pleasant walks along the Bayside Trail.

1984

By September 22, 1984, the trail huts were falling into disrepair. They were nearly a decade old and appear to have received little maintenance.

1994

On September 16, 1994, an Interagency Committee for Outdoor Recreation inspection found the trails open and in compliance with grant requirements.

2002

A letter to the editor described the poor trail condition and encampment issues; city officials reportedly responded with cleanup actions.

2010

National Park Service staff identified a possible conversion (closure) issue, flagging concerns that Tacoma had closed grant-funded public trail property.

2011

On March 19, 2011, RCO organized a walkthrough documenting closed access, no-trespassing signage, and related compliance issues.

Inspection slides: 2011 walkthrough deck.

A public workshop followed on March 31, 2011: workshop presentation.

2013

On October 3, 2013, RCO again inspected the trailheads near Stadium Way and documented ongoing compliance issues and blocked entrances.

Inspection deck: 2013 inspection slides.

2014

On October 3, 2014, Washington RCO sent an email to the Mayor’s office stating Tacoma remained in violation of obligations related to the Bayside Trails. Record: RCO email.

2015

Tacoma News Tribune ran stories on Tacoma gulches:

  • thenewstribune.com article
  • gulch project home
  • gulch maps

Environmental steward Robert Girvin continued restoration work in the gulch.

2018

An April 6, 2018 compliance report marked the Bayside Trails as closed, with action pending.

2022

In 2022, neighborhood restoration accelerated. Ben Lackey and other residents began walking, researching, clearing debris, and coordinating with long-time stewards and neighbors.

2023

In 2023, volunteers removed logs and invasives, reopened passable sections, organized work parties, and escalated city reporting related to closures, camps, and maintenance failures.

A major turning point came on July 22, 2023 when volunteers, Parks Tacoma, and others walked Garfield Gulch and much of the railroad grade, beginning a new period of public use and visibility.

In late 2023, WTA walkthroughs and work parties led to switchback and tread improvements in Garfield Gulch, though timelines and methods were frequently debated.

2024

In 2024, work parties continued, including vine cutting and mulch events. Community outreach increased through flyers, neighborhood organizing, and regular trail walks.

A July 24, 2024 city FAQ framed ongoing closure rationales: city FAQ document.

Parks and WTA activity continued through late 2024 with switchback and catwalk improvements in Garfield Gulch, while larger reopening questions remained.

2025

In 2025, WTA resumed catwalk and box-step work while public advocacy expanded. Community presentations, neighborhood walks, and online organizing significantly increased public attention.

Public records repository: document archive and email archive.

Additional references: Reddit discussion, r/baysidetrails, and campaign policy page.
